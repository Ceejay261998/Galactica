Stars

Nebulas are giant gas clouds of dust and gas in space. When they clump together by action of gravity, they accumulate more mass and gravity gets stronger and stronger. Eventually, they collapse under their own gravity and the core heats up. A star is born.

The duration of the life of a star depends mainly on its size. Massive stars shine more brightly and use up fuel quickly, while smaller ones lose their fuel more slowly. The little ones have been observing the cosmos for a longer time than the bigger ones.

In time, by becoming red giants or supergiants, stars burn their fuel. Depending on its size and mass, it will either go on a supernova- that is it will shed its outer layers in a bright explosion, or collapse into planetary nebulas.

Stars that undergo supernova can either become a neutron star, or a black hole. Those that became planetary nebulas condense, and become a white dwarf. After billions of years, all of these will cease to exist, resulting in the heat death of the universe.

Stars

Stars are the fundamental building blocks of the galaxies. They are the twinkles of light in the night-sky. For billions of years, these celestial bodies have been beacons of universal history, and will continue to be so in the next billions or more.

Galaxies

Spiral Galaxies

The most common type of galaxy. They have a rotating disc with
spiral arms that curve out from the center.

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies are oval in shape, with stars distributed uniformly throughout.
They contain less stars than spiral ones.

Irregular Galaxies

These are galaxies that don't have any standard shapes or forms.
Some are formed by the collision of galaxies themselves.
